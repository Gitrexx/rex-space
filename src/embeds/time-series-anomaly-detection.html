Time Series · Anomaly Detection

Spotting the abnormal in a stream of time

You're building anomaly detection for Negative Customer Impact — login attempts, transfer attempts, and other user-journey signals over time — so that when a journey starts misbehaving you catch it in minutes, not from an angry ticket. This module walks the whole toolkit, from the z-score your grandparents used to the foundation models of 2025, and tells you which parts actually earn their keep.

The one idea

Every anomaly detector, no matter how fancy, does the same three things:

  1. Model "normal." Learn what the signal usually looks like — its level, its daily and weekly rhythm, its typical jitter.
  2. Score the deviation. For each new point (or window), measure how far it sits from that model. That number is the anomaly score.
  3. Threshold. Decide how big a score is "too big" and raise a flag.

The entire field — statistical, classical ML, deep learning, foundation models — is just an escalating series of answers to step 1: how do you model normal? A z-score models it as a bell curve. An LSTM models it as "what I predicted the next value would be." A foundation model models it as "everything I learned from a billion other time series." Steps 2 and 3 barely change.

Key insight

Anomaly detection is not classification. You almost never have labels, the positive class is rare and shape-shifting, and yesterday's "abnormal" spike is today's Black Friday. So you don't learn "what an anomaly looks like" — you learn what normal looks like and flag anything that doesn't fit. That reframing is the whole game.

Three kinds of anomaly, three kinds of setup

Before choosing a method, name what you're hunting. The classic taxonomy (Chandola et al.):

  • Point anomaly — a single value that's wrong on its own. 3,000 login attempts in a minute when the norm is 40.
  • Contextual anomaly — a value that's normal in general but wrong for its context. 40 transfers/min is fine at 2pm, alarming at 3am. The value only looks wrong once you condition on time-of-day / day-of-week.
  • Collective anomaly — no single point is odd, but the sequence is. Login latency creeping up 2ms every minute for an hour — each step is unremarkable, the trend is the incident.

Your user-journey signals are riddled with all three, and mostly the contextual kind, because everything is seasonal (people log in during the day, transfer on paydays). A detector that ignores seasonality will page you every morning at 9am.

Deep dive — the axes that actually change your architecture

  • Univariate vs multivariate. One signal, or many correlated signals where the anomaly is a broken correlation (logins up, but successful-logins flat → something's failing silently)? Multivariate is harder and is where deep models start to pay off.
  • Supervised / semi-supervised / unsupervised. Almost all real deployments are unsupervised (no labels) or semi-supervised (train on a stretch you believe is clean, flag departures). True supervised anomaly detection is rare — if you had abundant labels you'd often just build a classifier.
  • Streaming vs batch. Do you score points online as they arrive (you do), or re-scan a historical window? Online forces causal features — no peeking at the future — and a model that updates as the baseline drifts.

Your dashboard shows 45 transfer attempts/min at 3am. The daily peak is 400/min at noon; the 3am norm is ~5/min. A plain global z-score over the raw series (mean ≈ 150) gives this point a z of about −2.2 and stays silent. What's going on?

  • The z-score is right — 45 is well within the normal range, so it's not an anomaly.
  • It's a contextual anomaly: 45 is ~9× the 3am norm, but a global model that ignores time-of-day dilutes it against the noon peak and misses it.
  • Z-scores can't detect spikes, only dips.
This is the defining failure of context-blind detectors. 45/min is a 9× surge for 3am — potentially a credential-stuffing run — but pooled against a series that routinely hits 400, it looks small. The fix: model normal conditional on context — deseasonalize first (STL, or a seasonal-naive baseline), then score the residual. Everything good about time-series anomaly detection starts with respecting seasonality.

1 · Statistical foundations

Old, transparent, cheap, and still the right first answer more often than anyone building a Transformer wants to admit.

Distance-from-center

  • Z-score: z = (x − μ) / σ. Flag |z| > 3. Assumes roughly Gaussian, stationary data. Breaks on skew, seasonality, and — critically — its own μ and σ get poisoned by the very outliers you're hunting.
  • Robust z-score (median + MAD): replace mean/std with median and median absolute deviation, MAD = median(|x − median(x)|), scaled by 1.4826 to match σ for normal data. One giant spike barely moves the median. This is the workhorse — reach for it before anything else.
  • Generalized ESD / Grubbs: a principled test for "how many outliers are in this batch," iteratively removing the most extreme point. Twitter's Seasonal Hybrid ESD (S-H-ESD) wraps this in an STL decomposition and made it famous for ops monitoring.

Decompose, then hunt the residual

The single most useful move for user-journey data: split the signal into trend + seasonality + residual (classic STL decomposition), then run your detector on the residual only. Seasonality is now the model's job, not the alert's. A 3am surge that vanished into a global z-score screams once the daily cycle is subtracted out.

Forecast, then score the error

Fit a forecaster to "normal," predict the next value, and treat the prediction error as the anomaly score. Big error = the future didn't match the past = something's off.

  • EWMA / exponential smoothing — a rolling weighted mean; cheap, online, great for level shifts. The band in the runnable JS below is an EWMA detector.
  • Holt-Winters — exponential smoothing with a seasonal term; a solid seasonal baseline in a few lines.
  • ARIMA / SARIMA — the classical linear forecaster; residuals-as-scores is a textbook detector. Heavier to fit and tune, and it assumes linearity.

Deep dive — control charts, CUSUM, and change-point detection

From statistical process control: Shewhart / EWMA / CUSUM charts were built exactly for "is this process still in control?" CUSUM accumulates deviations, so it's superb at catching slow drifts (collective anomalies) that a per-point threshold sleeps through — the "latency creeping up 2ms/min" case. Related but distinct: change-point detection (e.g. Bayesian online change-point detection, PELT) asks "did the regime shift?" rather than "is this one point weird?" For NCI you'll want both lenses — a spike detector for surges, a change-point / CUSUM lens for the quiet degradations.

When this tier wins

Univariate signal, clear seasonality, need explainability and sub-millisecond scoring, few or no labels. That describes most operational metrics. Robust z-score on STL residuals is a shockingly strong baseline — beat it before you reach for anything heavier.

The key mental shift from the statistical tier: you now engineer features — lags, rolling means, time-of-day, hour-over-hour deltas, ratios between signals — and let the model find the weird region. That's also the tier's ceiling: it's only as good as the features, and most of these methods are natively a-temporal (they'd shuffle your timestamps and never notice). You put the time back in by hand.

Deep dive — why Isolation Forest is the classical-ML default

Instead of profiling normal points (expensive) it exploits that anomalies are few and different, so a random binary split isolates them in fewer steps. The score is the average path length to isolate a point across an ensemble of random trees — short path ⇒ anomalous. It's linear-time, embarrassingly parallel, needs no distance metric or scaling, and handles many dimensions. For a multivariate NCI feature vector (attempts, successes, latency, error-rate, all with lag features) it's the natural "try this second" after robust z-scores on the individual signals.

3 · Deep learning

When the signal is high-dimensional, the seasonality is complex, and "normal" is a nonlinear manifold no hand-built feature captures.

Two big families, both still doing the same three things from the top of the page:

Reconstruction-based

Train a model to compress and rebuild normal data. It gets good at reconstructing normal and bad at reconstructing anything it never saw — so reconstruction error is the anomaly score.

  • Autoencoders / LSTM-autoencoders — the baseline; encode a window, decode it, score the error.
  • VAE — probabilistic version; gives a likelihood, not just an error.
  • USAD, TranAD — adversarially / transformer-trained reconstructors purpose-built for multivariate TSAD; strong on benchmarks, popular baselines.
  • GAN-based (MAD-GAN, TadGAN) — the generator learns the normal manifold; poorly reconstructed / low-support points are flagged.

Forecasting-based

Same "predict next, score the error" idea as ARIMA, but with a neural forecaster: LSTM/GRU, TCN, or a Transformer. More capacity for long-range and nonlinear seasonality; more data and tuning needed.

Deep dive — the Anomaly Transformer, TimesNet, and diffusion

  • Anomaly Transformer introduced association discrepancy: a normal point attends broadly across the series, an anomaly can only strongly associate with its immediate neighbors. That gap between a learned "prior" (local) attention and the "series" attention is itself the score — a genuinely time-series-native idea, not a borrowed NLP trick.
  • TimesNet reshapes a 1-D series into 2-D by its dominant periods and applies vision backbones, capturing multi-periodicity for general TS tasks including anomaly detection.
  • Diffusion models (e.g. ImDiffusion) frame detection as imputation: mask points, let a diffusion model reconstruct them from context; large imputation error ⇒ anomaly. A current, competitive line of work.
Reality check

Deep TSAD models look dominant on paper largely because of how the papers were scored (see Evaluation). On the cleaned-up TSB-AD benchmark, simple methods are frequently competitive with or better than elaborate deep models on many datasets. Deep learning earns its cost mainly on genuinely high-dimensional, correlation-structured problems — not on a single seasonal counter.

A reconstruction-based autoencoder is trained on three weeks of "normal" traffic that — unknown to you — contains a two-hour outage. At inference, similar outages produce low reconstruction error and go undetected. Why?

  • Autoencoders can't represent dips, only spikes.
  • The model learned to reconstruct the outage as "normal" because it was in the training set — semi-supervised detectors assume the training window is clean, and this one wasn't.
  • Reconstruction error is only valid for multivariate data.
This is contamination. Reconstruction/forecasting detectors are semi-supervised: they define normal as "whatever was in training." Anomalies in the training window get absorbed into "normal" and become invisible later. Mitigations: curate a known-clean window, use robust training, or iteratively remove high-error points and refit. It's the deep-learning echo of the z-score getting poisoned by its own outliers.

4 · Foundation models & GenAI — is there really anything here?

Short answer: yes, it's a real and fast-moving line as of 2025 — but it's mostly time-series foundation models, not ChatGPT reading your charts, and the hype is well ahead of the evidence for subtle anomalies.

Time-series foundation models (the substantive part)

Just as LLMs pretrain on text, a wave of models pretrain on huge corpora of time series and forecast zero-shot on series they've never seen: TimesFM (Google), Chronos (Amazon), Moirai (Salesforce), TimeGPT (Nixtla), Lag-Llama, IBM's Tiny Time Mixers / TSPulse. The anomaly-detection play is direct: use the zero-shot forecast as your model of normal, score the residual — no per-signal training. For a project like NCI with hundreds of heterogeneous journey metrics, "one model, no per-metric fitting" is the genuinely attractive promise.

Purpose-built zero-shot anomaly foundation models are now appearing too — e.g. TimeRCD, which pretrains on synthetic data around a "relative context discrepancy" signal (an anomaly is where a window's relationship to its context breaks) and reports beating general forecasting foundation models at zero-shot TSAD. Early, but pointed in a clear direction.

LLMs (the overhyped part)

Can a general LLM detect anomalies from a series dropped into the prompt? A careful ICLR 2025 study, "Can LLMs Understand Time Series Anomalies?", found the honest answer is mostly no:

  • LLMs understand a series better as an image than as text tokens — contradicting the "LLMs are just sequence models" intuition.
  • Asking them to reason step-by-step did not help.
  • They catch trivial, obvious anomalies; there's no evidence they catch subtle real-world ones. Behavior varies wildly by model.

Where LLMs do add value is around the detector, not as the detector: explaining a flagged anomaly in plain language, correlating it with deploy logs / incident tickets, drafting the alert, and agentic triage — e.g. AnomaMind-style tool-augmented reasoning that calls real detectors and stitches together an explanation. That's the realistic near-term GenAI role for NCI: a numeric detector fires, an LLM turns "residual_z=8.1 on transfer_success at 14:03" into "transfer success dropped 60% right after the 14:00 payments deploy — likely related."

For NCI you consider replacing your per-metric detectors with "paste each series into GPT and ask if it's anomalous." Based on the 2025 evidence, the best characterization is:

  • Great idea — LLMs are state-of-the-art at time-series anomaly detection.
  • Risky as the detector — LLMs only reliably catch obvious anomalies and vary by model — but valuable as an explanation/triage layer on top of numeric detectors.
  • Impossible — LLMs cannot process numbers at all.
The research consensus in 2025: general LLMs are unreliable primary detectors for subtle anomalies, but strong at the human-facing layer — explanation, correlation, and agentic triage. The foundation-model angle worth piloting for detection is a time-series foundation model (TimesFM/Chronos/TimeGPT/TimeRCD) used zero-shot, not a chat LLM.

The part everyone gets wrong: evaluation

This deserves its own section because a huge fraction of published "SOTA" is an artifact of broken scoring — and if you copy those habits your NCI dashboard will look great and catch nothing.

The point-adjustment scandal

For years, papers used point adjustment (PA): if a detector flags any single point inside a ground-truth anomaly segment, the entire segment is counted as correctly detected. It sounds reasonable and is catastrophically permissive. Researchers showed a random anomaly score achieves state-of-the-art PA-F1 — i.e. the metric rewarded noise. Any result that leans on PA-F1 should be treated as unproven.

What to use instead

  • Range/segment-aware metrics that reward correct extent and timing, not one lucky hit — and VUS-PR (Volume Under the Surface of the PR curve), which is threshold-robust and is the headline metric recommended by the TSB-AD benchmark (NeurIPS 2024).
  • Detection latency — for ops, how fast you flagged matters as much as whether you did. A detector that's right 20 minutes late didn't help the customer.
  • Alert volume / precision at your real budget — how many pages per week at the recall you need? This is the number your on-call team actually feels.
Pitfall

Beware the benchmarks themselves. The TSB-AD authors ("The Elephant in the Room") documented mislabeled ground truth, trivially-easy series, and unrealistic setups across the field's classic datasets (parts of SMD, SMAP/MSL, Yahoo, etc.). Trust cleaned benchmarks (TSB-AD, TSB-UAD) and, above all, a labeled slice of your own NCI incidents over a leaderboard number.

A vendor's detector reports 0.95 PA-F1 on a public benchmark. What's the right prior?

  • Excellent — 0.95 F1 means it'll catch 95% of your incidents.
  • Deeply skeptical — point-adjusted F1 is so permissive that even random scores hit SOTA; ask for VUS-PR / range-based metrics and a test on your own data.
  • It depends only on whether they used a neural network.
PA-F1 is the single biggest red flag in the field. Because flagging one point credits an entire segment, the number is nearly decoupled from real localization quality. Insist on threshold-robust, range-aware metrics (VUS-PR, range-based precision/recall) and, ideally, an evaluation on a labeled window of your own traffic.
