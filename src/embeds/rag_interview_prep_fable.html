module 00 / orientation

The 2026 RAG landscape

Why interviewers ask: they want to know if you see RAG as a search-engineering problem or as "stuff chunks into a prompt."

What "naive RAG" means — and why the term exists

The 2023 recipe: split documents into fixed ~512-token chunks → embed each chunk → store in a vector DB → at query time, embed the query, take top-k by cosine similarity, paste into the prompt, generate. It works in demos and degrades in production. Industry post-mortems from 2025 put the share of enterprise RAG projects that critically struggle or fail in year one at roughly 70–80% — and the dominant root cause is almost never the LLM. It's the retriever feeding it irrelevant, incomplete, or badly-ranked context.

The framing that signals seniority: RAG is information engineering, not prompt engineering. The generator is bounded by what retrieval gives it. Most of the techniques in this manual exist to attack one of three gaps:

  • Representation gap — a single chunk vector can't capture everything a chunk is "about"; meaning gets averaged away (chunking, contextual enrichment, multi-vector, late interaction).
  • Query–document gap — questions don't look like answers; vocabulary and form mismatch (query transformation, HyDE, hybrid sparse+dense).
  • Reasoning gap — some answers live in relationships across chunks, not inside any one of them (GraphRAG, multi-hop, agentic loops).

The modern pipeline — two lanes

offline / index parse→ chunk→ enrich (context, metadata)→ embed (dense + sparse)→ index (HNSW + inverted)
online / query transform query→ hybrid retrieve→ fuse (RRF)→ rerank→ assemble context→ generate + cite→ evaluate

Each stage is a module below. Two design principles run through all of them:

  • Recall first, precision second. First-stage retrieval is tuned for recall (cast a wide, cheap net — top 50–150 candidates); a second stage (reranker) buys precision. You cannot rerank what you never retrieved.
  • Decouple the retrieval unit from the generation unit. What you match on (a sentence, a proposition, a contextualized chunk) doesn't have to be what you hand to the LLM (the parent section, the whole doc).

The 2026 production floor

The community-consensus minimum for a serious deployment: hybrid retrieval (dense embeddings + BM25) fused with Reciprocal Rank Fusion, followed by a cross-encoder reranker, wrapped in an evaluation harness with separate retrieval and generation metrics. Pure single-vector top-k is now treated as a prototype, because single-vector similarity has a hard representational ceiling — it finds what is similar to the query, not necessarily what is relevant to answering it.

your project, reframed

You already run dense + BM25 fused with RRF on Elasticsearch. That is the 2026 floor — most teams arrived there in 2025 after their pure-vector v1 failed. And document-level embedding over short, self-contained documents is not "skipping chunking"; it's the degenerate-but-correct case where the document already fits the optimal retrieval unit. Present both as deliberate decisions, then pivot: "here's what I'd change the moment the corpus stops looking like that."

interview lens — "walk me through your RAG system"

Structure: corpus properties → retrieval-unit decision → retrieval architecture → what you measured → what you'd do next. Lead with corpus properties; it shows the design followed the data.

My corpus was short, self-contained web documents, so the document itself was the right retrieval unit — chunking would have fragmented already-atomic content. I ran hybrid retrieval, BM25 plus dense embeddings fused with RRF, because the two channels fail differently: BM25 nails exact terms and IDs, dense handles paraphrase. RRF let me fuse them without score normalization. If the corpus grew long-form documents tomorrow, the first changes would be a chunking layer with parent-document retrieval and a cross-encoder reranker — and I'd justify each with recall@k and nDCG on a golden set, not vibes.

module 01 / ingestion

Chunking

Why interviewers ask: chunking is where most retrieval quality is silently won or lost, and it has no universal answer — perfect for probing judgment.

Why chunk at all

Three independent pressures force the decision:

  • Embedding capacity. A single vector over a long document averages many topics into mush — "embedding dilution." Retrieval precision drops because the vector is about everything and therefore nothing.
  • Retrieval precision. The LLM needs the answer-bearing span, not 40 pages around it. Smaller units localize relevance.
  • Context budget. Even with long-context models, tokens cost money and latency, and relevance signal degrades as context fills with noise.

The core tension: small chunks retrieve precisely but lose surrounding meaning; large chunks preserve meaning but retrieve fuzzily and waste budget. Every strategy below is a different resolution of that tension.

Chunk size has no universal best value: 200–800 tokens covers most production systems, tuned per corpus on a golden set. The honest interview answer is always "I'd sweep size × overlap and pick by recall@k and downstream answer quality, because optimal size depends on how the corpus packs information."

Decoupling tricks — small-to-big, parent-document, sentence-window retrieve small, generate big

These exploit the recall/precision asymmetry directly: match on a small precise unit, but return a larger unit to the LLM.

  • Parent-document retrieval: index child chunks (say 200 tokens) that each point to a parent (say 1,000 tokens or the full doc). Search hits children; the parent is what enters the prompt. LangChain ParentDocumentRetriever; LlamaIndex auto-merging retriever (merges children back into the parent when enough siblings match).
  • Sentence-window: embed single sentences; at synthesis time swap each hit for the sentence ± a window of its neighbors (LlamaIndex SentenceWindowNodeParser + MetadataReplacementPostProcessor).
  • Proposition indexing: have an LLM rewrite text into atomic, self-contained factual statements and index those — maximal precision, real preprocessing cost.

This family is the standard cure when you observe "retrieval finds the right place but the chunk alone isn't enough to answer."

Context-preserving embeddings — Contextual Retrieval vs Late Chunking the 2024–25 upgrades

Both attack the same disease: a chunk ripped out of its document loses referents. "Its more than 3.85 million inhabitants…" embeds poorly when "Berlin" lives two paragraphs up. "The company's revenue grew 3%" is unretrievable when the company and quarter are named elsewhere.

Contextual Retrieval (Anthropic, 2024). At index time, an LLM sees the whole document plus each chunk and writes 50–100 tokens situating that chunk ("This chunk is from ACME's Q2-2023 SEC filing; previous-quarter revenue was $314M…"). The context is prepended before embedding — and before BM25 indexing too (contextual BM25). Anthropic reported a ~49% reduction in top-20 retrieval failure rate, ~67% combined with reranking. Cost is one LLM pass per chunk at index time, made tolerable by prompt caching (the document is cached; only the chunk varies).

Late Chunking (Jina, 2024). Reorders the pipeline instead of adding text: run the entire document through a long-context embedding model first, so every token's embedding is conditioned on full-document context; then apply chunk boundaries and mean-pool each span into its chunk vector. The pronoun's token embedding already "knows" it refers to Berlin. No LLM calls, no extra stored text — but it requires a long-context embedder and the gains are real but smaller.

pitfalls that come up in interviews
  • Splitting tables or code blocks mid-structure — parse them out as atomic units instead.
  • Losing heading context — prepend the header path ("Returns Policy > International") to each chunk.
  • Overlap duplication — overlapping chunks both retrieved waste context; dedupe near-identical spans at assembly.

interview lens — "how would you chunk X?"

Pattern: inspect structure first → choose a unit aligned with answer-bearing spans → decouple retrieval unit from generation unit → name the experiment that decides it.

Before picking a number I'd look at the documents: do they have structure I can respect — headers, sections, tables? I'd chunk along structure, carry the heading path into each chunk, and keep tables atomic. If chunks lose context when isolated, I'd add contextual enrichment — Anthropic-style LLM context if quality justifies index-time cost, late chunking if I need it cheap. And I'd decide chunk size empirically: sweep size and overlap, score recall@k against a golden set. My own project sat at the boundary case — documents short enough to be their own chunks — which is why document-level embedding was the right call there.

module 02 / query side

Query transformation

Why interviewers ask: it tests whether you understand that the query, not just the index, is a design surface — and whether you know what each trick costs.

The problem: questions don't look like answers

Embedding retrieval matches the query vector against chunk vectors. But user queries are short, underspecified, conversational, and phrased as questions, while the corpus is long, declarative, and domain-toned. Every technique below reshapes the query to close that gap — and every one of them adds an LLM call, so the meta-skill is knowing when each is worth its latency.

Rewriting & conversational condensation the unglamorous workhorse

In multi-turn chat, "what about the second one?" retrieves nothing. The standard first step of conversational RAG: an LLM condenses chat history + the new turn into a standalone query ("what is the refund window for the Pro plan?") before retrieval. Also covers spelling/jargon normalization and stripping filler. Cheap, near-universal in chat products, and the thing most candidates forget to mention when asked to design a support bot.

Multi-query & RAG-Fusion cast several nets, fuse with RRF

Multi-query expansion: an LLM writes 3–5 paraphrases/perspectives of the query; each is retrieved independently; results are unioned. Counters embedding sensitivity to phrasing — one query's blind spot is another's hit. (LangChain MultiQueryRetriever.)

RAG-Fusion = multi-query + Reciprocal Rank Fusion across the result lists. Each document scores Σ 1/(k + ranki) over the lists it appears in (k≈60). Documents that rank well across multiple phrasings float up — a consensus effect. You already use RRF to fuse BM25 + dense; RAG-Fusion is the same operator applied across query variants. One operator, two jobs — a satisfying thing to point out in an interview.

Cost: N× retrieval fan-out + one LLM call. Worth it for ambiguous or multi-faceted queries; wasted on crisp lookups — which is an argument for routing (below).

HyDE — Hypothetical Document Embeddings search with a fake answer

Mechanism: ask an LLM to write a hypothetical answer to the query (it may be wrong in facts — doesn't matter), embed that hypothetical document, and search with its vector instead of the query's.

Why it works: a fake answer is shaped like a real answer — declarative, domain vocabulary, right length. Answer-to-answer similarity is geometrically easier than question-to-answer similarity. You're moving the search point from "question space" into "document space."

When it shines: zero-shot domains with no fine-tuned retriever; short vague queries against verbose technical corpora.

When it hurts: the LLM hallucinates a hypothetical about the wrong topic (entity confusion drags retrieval off-course); precise lookups (IDs, error codes) where BM25 already wins; latency-sensitive paths — it's a full generation before retrieval even starts.

Step-back & decomposition abstract up, or split apart

Step-back prompting: derive a more general question first ("What were Einstein's positions 1905–1915?" ← "what physics principles govern this setup?"), retrieve for both the step-back and original query, answer from the union. Helps when the corpus discusses principles but the user asks instances.

Decomposition: split a multi-hop or comparative question into sub-questions answered independently (possibly sequentially, where sub-answer 1 feeds sub-question 2), then synthesize. "Compare LangChain's and LlamaIndex's approach to streaming" → two retrievals, one synthesis. This is the static ancestor of agentic RAG — same idea, fixed control flow. (LlamaIndex SubQuestionQueryEngine.)

Routing & self-query spend transforms only where needed

Routing: a cheap classifier (often a small LLM with structured output) picks the path per query: which index (product docs vs tickets vs SQL), which strategy (direct lookup vs multi-query vs decomposition), or whether to retrieve at all (chitchat doesn't need the vector DB). Routing is what keeps the average-case latency flat while the worst case gets the full arsenal.

Self-query / metadata extraction: the LLM parses the natural-language query into a semantic part + structured filters ("reviews of Dune after 2023 with rating > 4" → filter year>2023, rating>4). The filter runs as a real predicate in the vector store, not as embedding soup. Essential whenever metadata exists.

interview lens

Always tie a transform to a diagnosed failure: don't deploy HyDE "because it's cool" — deploy it because eval showed paraphrase-mismatch failures. And mention the router: transforms cost latency, so classify first, transform selectively.

I'd treat query transformation as a menu the router orders from, not a fixed pipeline. Conversational condensation is always on in chat. If failure analysis shows vocabulary mismatch, multi-query plus RRF is my first move because I already trust RRF as a fusion operator. HyDE I'd reserve for zero-shot domains, and I'd A/B it — it can drag retrieval off-topic when the hypothetical hallucinates the wrong entity. Each transform is one more LLM call, so the question is always: which queries earn it?

module 03 / index side

Indexing & retrieval

Why interviewers ask: this is the systems-engineering core — recall/latency/memory trade-offs you can only discuss if you know what the data structures actually do.

Dense retrieval: embeddings + ANN indexes

Exact nearest-neighbor search is O(N) per query; approximate nearest neighbor (ANN) indexes trade a sliver of recall for orders-of-magnitude speed. The two families you must be able to contrast:

HNSW — the default graph-based, RAM-hungry, fast

Hierarchical Navigable Small World: a multi-layer proximity graph. Sparse top layers let search "teleport" across the space; each descent refines locally via greedy best-first walk. The knobs, and what they actually trade:

  • M — edges per node. Higher M → denser graph → better recall, more RAM, slower builds.
  • efConstruction — candidate-list width at build time. Higher → better graph quality, slower indexing. Pay once.
  • efSearch — candidate-list width at query time. The live recall↔latency dial; tune it per SLA without rebuilding.

Costs: the whole graph (and usually vectors) lives in RAM; deletes are awkward (tombstones, periodic rebuilds). Elasticsearch's dense_vector kNN — your stack — is HNSW underneath, so you can claim hands-on adjacency.

IVF and PQ — cluster + compress when RAM is the constraint

IVF (inverted file): k-means the vectors into nlist cells; at query time probe only the nprobe nearest cells. Recall is capped by whether the true neighbor's cell got probed — raising nprobe buys recall with latency.

PQ (product quantization): split each vector into m sub-vectors, replace each with the ID of its nearest centroid from a small codebook → vectors compress from KBs to tens of bytes. Distances are approximated from codebook lookups; accuracy drops accordingly. IVF-PQ = both: probe a few cells, scan compressed codes, optionally re-rank survivors with full-precision vectors.

The clean contrast for interviews: HNSW spends memory to buy speed and recall; IVF-PQ spends recall to buy memory. Billion-scale or disk-resident corpora (DiskANN-style) push you toward the IVF/compression family; ≤ a few million vectors with latency SLAs → HNSW; ≤ ~100k with strict exactness needs → honest brute force is often fine.

Embedding model choices in 2026 what to know by name

Don't memorize leaderboards; know the decision axes: multilingual need, context length, multimodality, hosting constraints, and dimension cost. Names worth recognizing: Qwen3-Embedding (open-weight, tops multilingual MTEB), BGE-M3 (open-source workhorse; emits dense + sparse + multi-vector from one model), Voyage and Cohere Embed v4 (commercial retrieval-tuned; v4 does long-context + multimodal), OpenAI text-embedding-3, Gemini Embedding. Two production levers interviewers like:

  • Matryoshka (MRL) embeddings — trained so prefixes of the vector are usable; truncate 3072→512 dims for big storage cuts with minor recall loss; coarse-search truncated, re-rank full.
  • Quantization — int8 or binary vector storage (binary + Hamming distance for first pass, rescore with floats). Cheap to mention, very production-real.
  • MTEB caution — public-benchmark overfitting is real; always validate on your retrieval task before swapping models. Also: changing embedding models means re-embedding the entire corpus — plan migrations.

Sparse retrieval: BM25 and learned sparse

BM25 — why it refuses to die exact match, zero training

Term-frequency × inverse-document-frequency with two fixes that make it robust: TF saturation (the 10th occurrence of a term adds less than the 2nd, controlled by k1) and length normalization (long documents don't win by volume, controlled by b). Its enduring superpowers: exact identifiers, error codes, product SKUs, rare jargon, names — precisely where embeddings blur. Zero training, interpretable, fast on an inverted index.

SPLADE — neural retrieval in an inverted index learned sparse expansion

A BERT-family model maps text onto a ~30k-term vocabulary vector: most weights zero (sparsity regularization), nonzero weights are learned term importances — and crucially the model performs expansion, activating related terms that never appear in the text ("laptop" lights up "notebook," "computer"). You get semantic matching inside ordinary inverted-index infrastructure (Elasticsearch ELSER is this idea). Trade-offs: inference cost to encode, fatter postings lists than BM25, but solves vocabulary mismatch without dense infrastructure. Strong third channel in hybrid setups.

Hybrid retrieval & fusion — your home turf, one level deeper

Score fusion vs rank fusion (RRF) why RRF won by default

BM25 scores are unbounded; cosine sits in [-1,1] — adding them raw is meaningless. Score fusion (convex combination α·dense + (1−α)·sparse) needs per-channel normalization (min-max or z-score per result list), and α becomes a tunable that drifts across query types. RRF sidesteps scales entirely: score(d) = Σ 1/(k + ranki(d)). Properties worth saying out loud:

  • Scale-free — only ranks matter, so heterogeneous channels (BM25, dense, SPLADE) fuse with no calibration.
  • k dampens tail influence — with k=60, the gap between rank 1 and 2 is small; k controls how top-heavy fusion is. Small k → trust heads; large k → diffuse consensus.
  • Limitation — rank-only fusion discards score magnitude: a 0.95-confident dense hit and a 0.51 one fuse identically if ranked the same. Weighted RRF (per-channel multipliers) is the common patch; a reranker downstream is the better one.
ColBERT & late interaction multi-vector precision

Instead of one vector per chunk, keep one per token. At query time, each query token finds its best-matching document token (MaxSim) and the sums are aggregated. Fine-grained term-level matching with bi-encoder-style precomputation — quality approaching cross-encoders at retrieval-friendly latency. The price: storage blows up (hundreds of vectors per chunk; mitigated by compression/pooling in ColBERTv2/PLAID). The same late-interaction idea powers ColPali for document images (module 05). Position it as the middle point on the precision↔cost line: single-vector < late interaction < cross-encoder.

Metadata filtering pre-filter vs post-filter

Post-filtering (retrieve top-k, then filter) can return fewer than k — or zero — when the filter is selective. Pre-filtering applies the predicate during ANN traversal; correct, but highly selective filters degrade HNSW graph connectivity (the walk keeps hitting filtered-out nodes). Production engines build filter-aware indexes (e.g., partitioning by tenant). Mentioning this distinction signals you've operated, not just demoed. ACL enforcement (module 08's enterprise scenario) is exactly a pre-filter problem: retrieval-time permission filtering, never post-hoc.

interview lens

I think of first-stage retrieval as a recall machine with three channels — BM25 for exact terms, dense for paraphrase, optionally learned-sparse like SPLADE for expansion — fused with RRF because it's scale-free. On the index side it's a memory/recall/latency triangle: HNSW when RAM allows and latency matters, IVF-PQ when scale forces compression, brute force below ~100k vectors. efSearch is the dial I tune live against the latency SLA. Precision is not first-stage's job — that's the reranker's.

module 04 / precision stage

Reranking & context engineering

Why interviewers ask: the rerank stage is the highest-leverage, lowest-risk upgrade to most pipelines — knowing exactly why shows you understand model architecture, not just APIs.

Bi-encoder vs cross-encoder — the architectural reason rerankers win

A bi-encoder (your embedding model) encodes query and document separately; relevance is a dot product of two independently-formed vectors. Fast — documents embed offline — but the model never sees query and document together, so it can't resolve fine interactions ("does this 'apple' mean the company given this query?").

A cross-encoder feeds the concatenated (query, document) pair through the transformer; every query token attends to every document token; output is a single calibrated relevance score. Far more accurate — and O(candidates) full forward passes per query, so it can't be the first stage over millions of docs.

Hence the canonical two-stage funnel: cheap high-recall first stage retrieves 50–150 candidates → cross-encoder rescores them → top 5–10 enter the prompt. Reranking also quietly solves your RRF limitation: heterogeneous channels fused by rank get re-scored on one calibrated scale.

The reranker menu in 2026 cross-encoders, LLM rerankers
  • Cross-encoder services/models: Cohere Rerank 3.5 (API, multilingual), BGE-reranker-v2-m3 (open-source default), Qwen3-Reranker, Jina Reranker, mxbai-rerank. Latency: tens of ms for dozens of candidates on GPU.
  • LLM-as-reranker: prompt an LLM to score candidates (pointwise) or to order a list (listwise, RankGPT-style with sliding windows over the candidate list). Highest quality and most expensive; pragmatic at low QPS or for offline label generation to train a cheap cross-encoder.
  • Late-interaction rerank: ColBERT-style MaxSim as a middle stage when full cross-encoding is too slow.

Knobs that matter: how many candidates in (recall ceiling), how many out (context budget), score threshold to drop everything when nothing is relevant — feeding confident garbage to the LLM is how hallucinations get authoritative.

Context assembly — lost in the middle, compression, budgeting what happens after rerank
  • Lost in the middle (Liu et al.): LLM attention is U-shaped over long contexts — information buried mid-context is used worst. Mitigations: pass fewer, better chunks; place the strongest evidence first and last; don't max the window just because you can.
  • Contextual compression: shrink retrieved text before the prompt — extractive (keep only relevant sentences; embedding-filter or LLM extractor) or abstractive/token-level (LLMLingua-style). Trades a small compute cost for a cleaner, cheaper prompt.
  • Dedup & diversity: overlapping chunks and near-duplicates waste budget; MMR (maximal marginal relevance) balances relevance against redundancy when selecting the final set.
  • Structured assembly: delimit sources, attach IDs/titles for citation, order deliberately. The prompt is an engineered artifact, not a paste buffer.
interview lens

My mental model is a funnel: stage one is a recall machine, stage two is a precision machine. The cross-encoder earns its latency because it's the first component that actually reads the query and document together — full cross-attention — which is architecturally why it beats any similarity of independently-computed vectors. I'd retrieve 100, rerank to 5–8, set a relevance floor so the system can say 'nothing relevant found' instead of generating from noise, and I'd watch nDCG before/after to prove the stage pays rent.

module 05 / architectures

Advanced RAG architectures

Why interviewers ask: anyone can recite the pipeline; the differentiator is knowing which architecture matches which failure mode — and what each one costs.

Organize them by the question they answer. Self-RAG / CRAG: "what if retrieval is wrong or unnecessary?" RAPTOR / GraphRAG family: "what if the answer spans many chunks?" Agentic RAG: "what if one fixed pipeline can't fit every query?" Multimodal: "what if the knowledge isn't text?"

Self-RAG — the model critiques its own retrieval reflection tokens

A fine-tuned LM emits special reflection tokens during generation: Retrieve (do I need retrieval for this segment, or can I answer parametrically?), ISREL (is this passage relevant?), ISSUP (is my generated segment supported by the passage?), ISUSE (is the output useful?). Generation proceeds segment-by-segment, scoring candidate continuations by these self-critiques — adaptive retrieval plus built-in groundedness checking.

Honest assessment for interviews: the trained-token version is research-grade (you must fine-tune the generator), but the pattern — decide-to-retrieve, grade relevance, verify support — is widely reproduced with prompted judges in production systems. Cite it as the conceptual origin of "reflective RAG."

CRAG — Corrective RAG grade, refine, or go to the web

Plug-and-play, no generator fine-tuning. A lightweight retrieval evaluator (small fine-tuned model or prompted LLM) grades retrieved docs and triggers one of three actions:

  • Correct → confidence high: refine the evidence via decompose-then-recompose — split docs into strips, score each, keep only relevant strips (intra-document noise filtering).
  • Incorrect → all docs look wrong: discard them and fall back to web search (with query rewriting) for fresh evidence.
  • Ambiguous → mix both: refined strips + web results.

This is the canonical "freshness + robustness" answer: stale index, breaking-news query, knowledge gaps. It's also the textbook LangGraph tutorial graph — grade node, conditional edge, rewrite node — which makes it perfect Code Lab material (you'll wire it there).

RAPTOR — recursive summary tree retrieval at multiple abstraction levels

Build time: embed leaf chunks → cluster them (soft clustering, GMM) → LLM-summarize each cluster → embed the summaries → cluster those → repeat, yielding a tree whose upper nodes are progressively more abstract summaries. Query time: retrieve across all levels ("collapsed tree"), so detail questions hit leaves and thematic questions ("what are the main arguments of this book?") hit high-level summaries.

It attacks the failure where naive RAG fetches three random fragments of a 300-page document and pretends that's "the themes." Costs: heavy index-time LLM summarization; updates require re-clustering branches. Mention it for long single documents (books, lengthy reports) and as the tree-shaped cousin of GraphRAG's community summaries.

GraphRAG (Microsoft) — entities, communities, global questions the heavyweight

Index: LLM extracts entities + typed relations (+ claims) from every chunk → assemble a knowledge graph → run Leiden community detection to find clusters of related entities, hierarchically → LLM writes a community summary per cluster at each level. This is the expensive part: an LLM pass over the whole corpus plus summarization — indexing cost is the #1 practical objection.

Query modes:

  • Local search — entity-anchored: find query entities, expand to neighbors/claims/source chunks. For "tell me about X and its relationships."
  • Global search — map-reduce over community summaries: each relevant community answers, answers get reduced into one. For corpus-level "what are the main themes / who are the key players?" questions that pure vector RAG structurally cannot answer (no chunk contains "the themes").
  • DRIFT search — hybrid that starts global, drills local.

Decision rule: GraphRAG earns its cost when queries are relational or global ("how is A connected to B?", "summarize across everything") and the corpus is relatively static. For needle-in-haystack lookups it's expensive overkill.

LightRAG & HippoRAG — graph benefits, lighter bills the pragmatic graph family

LightRAG: builds an entity/relation graph but skips community clustering. Indexes nodes and edges as key-value entries (key: short label; value: summary). At query time an LLM extracts dual-level keywords — low-level (concrete entities) matched against node keys, high-level (themes) matched against edge/relation keys — then enriches with one-hop neighbors. Incremental updates are cheap (no global re-clustering), a direct answer to GraphRAG's static-corpus weakness.

HippoRAG (1 & 2): inspired by hippocampal indexing theory of human memory. Index: OpenIE-style triple extraction → schemaless KG with synonym edges (passages stay linked to their entities). Query: extract query entities → use them as seeds for Personalized PageRank over the graph — activation spreads through the network and lights up multi-hop-connected passages in a single retrieval step, no iterative LLM hops. Reported as dramatically cheaper than iterative multi-hop retrieval. HippoRAG 2 deepens passage integration alongside the graph.

Interview value: shows you know the graph space has a cost spectrum — Microsoft GraphRAG (heavy, global summaries) → LightRAG (dual-level, incremental) → HippoRAG (PPR, single-step multi-hop).

Agentic RAG — retrieval becomes a tool the 2025–26 default for hard queries

Invert the architecture: instead of a fixed pipeline with the LLM at the end, an LLM agent sits in a loop — plan → act (call a tool) → observe → repeat — and retrieval is just one tool alongside web search, SQL, calculators, other indexes. The agent decides whether to retrieve, which source, with what query, and whether results are good enough or warrant another round (self-correction loops subsume CRAG's grade-and-retry as a special case).

  • Patterns: single ReAct agent with tools; router agent → specialist retrievers; planner/executor; multi-agent (researcher + critic + writer). "Deep research" products are agentic RAG with web-scale tools and long budgets.
  • What it buys: multi-source synthesis, multi-hop reasoning, query-adaptive strategy, self-correction.
  • What it costs: latency (many LLM round-trips), tokens, and — the senior-engineer point — variance: agent trajectories are nondeterministic, harder to evaluate, debug, and bound. Production answer: agentic where queries demand it, behind a router; static pipeline for the 80% of simple queries; cap tool-call budgets; trace everything.

Orchestration is exactly what LangGraph and LlamaIndex Workflows exist for — module 06.

Multimodal RAG — when knowledge isn't text ColPali & vision-native retrieval

Two competing strategies for visually rich documents (slide decks, scanned PDFs, financial reports full of tables and charts):

  • Parse-to-text: OCR/layout-parse everything into text (+ LLM-generated descriptions of figures), then ordinary text RAG. Mature, searchable, but lossy — complex tables and charts mangle.
  • Vision-native retrieval: embed page images directly with a VLM. ColPali applies ColBERT-style late interaction over page-image patch embeddings — retrieval "sees" layout, tables, charts without parsing. 2025–26 brought production-grade multimodal embedders/rerankers (Qwen3-VL-Embedding/Reranker, Cohere Embed v4 multimodal, voyage-multimodal) and this went mainstream for enterprise documents.

Generation side: a VLM receives retrieved page images (or image + extracted text) as context. Decision axis: parsing pipelines when text dominates and you need fine-grained citation; vision-native when layout/visuals carry the meaning.

interview lens

I'd pick architecture by failure mode, not fashion. If eval shows answers missing because evidence spans documents, that's the graph family — full GraphRAG if I need global summaries and the corpus is static, LightRAG or HippoRAG if it updates often or budget is tight. If the failure is stale or unreliable retrieval, that's CRAG's grade-and-fallback. If query diversity is the problem, I put an agent behind a router with a tool-call budget — agentic only for the queries that earn it, because every loop iteration is latency and variance I have to own in production.

module 06 / tooling

Frameworks: LangChain, LangGraph, LlamaIndex

Why interviewers ask: framework fluency is a proxy for "has built more than one of these." You haven't used them — so learn the concepts and own the trade-off of not using them.

LangChain — and what changed in 1.0

LangChain's historical pitch: standard interfaces over models, retrievers, vector stores, plus composition. LangChain 1.0 (Oct 2025) was a major refocus you should know about:

  • create_agent is now the standard way to build agents — it replaced langgraph.prebuilt.create_react_agent and the long-deprecated AgentExecutor. It runs a tool-calling (ReAct-style) loop on LangGraph's durable runtime under the hood.
  • Middleware — composable hooks into the agent loop (before/after model calls and tool calls): summarize long histories, human-in-the-loop approval, PII scrubbing, dynamic prompts. This is LangChain's answer to "the abstraction was too rigid."
  • Slimmed namespace — legacy chains moved to langchain-classic; standard content_blocks across providers; no breaking changes promised until 2.0.
  • LCEL (the | pipe syntax over Runnables) remains the idiom for composing simple chains: retriever | prompt | llm | parser. Still what most RAG quickstarts look like — the Code Lab drills it.

LangGraph — when the loop needs to be a graph

A lower-level orchestration runtime: you define a typed state, nodes (functions that read/update state), and edges — including conditional edges and cycles, which classic DAG pipelines can't express. Built for production agents: durable execution (checkpointing — a crashed run resumes where it stopped), persistence-backed memory, human-in-the-loop interrupts, time-travel debugging. Reached 1.0 alongside LangChain.

The decision rule the LangChain team itself gives: start with create_agent; drop to LangGraph when you need explicit custom control flow — multi-agent topologies, deterministic stages mixed with agentic ones, approval gates. CRAG's grade→branch→rewrite loop is the canonical teaching example.

LlamaIndex — the data-first framework

  • Ingestion: SimpleDirectoryReader + 300+ LlamaHub connectors → Document objects → node parsers (SentenceSplitter, SentenceWindowNodeParser, semantic splitter) → Node objects with metadata/relationships. LlamaParse is the hosted parser for messy PDFs (tables!).
  • Indexing: VectorStoreIndex (plus property-graph, summary, keyword indexes) over any vector store; StorageContext for persistence.
  • Querying: index.as_query_engine() wraps retriever → node postprocessors (rerankers live here) → response synthesizer. Synthesis modes are a classic interview detail: refine (sequential: one LLM call per node, each refining the previous answer), compact (default: stuff as many nodes per call as fit, then refine across calls — fewer calls), tree_summarize (bottom-up recursive summarization — good for "summarize everything").
  • Composition: router query engines, SubQuestionQueryEngine (decomposition), chat engines (conversation + condensation). Workflows — event-driven steps (@step methods consuming/emitting typed events) — are now the recommended way to build anything non-trivial, replacing monolithic engine assembly; agents (FunctionAgent/ReActAgent) ride on top. llama-deploy serves workflows as services.

Choosing — and the "no framework" position you already hold

owning the "I used Elasticsearch directly" answer

Don't apologize for it — defend it, then show range. Frameworks buy velocity and integrations at the price of abstraction depth: more layers to debug, version churn, magic defaults you must audit anyway. For a stable, simple pipeline — one retrieval pattern, one index, your exact fusion logic — a thin client against ES is less code than the framework adapter, and you own every line on the latency path.

We deliberately skipped frameworks: our pipeline was one stable pattern — hybrid search with RRF — and Elasticsearch exposes that natively, so a framework would have added an abstraction layer over an API we already controlled, plus dependency churn on our critical path. The trade-off I'd reconsider as scope grows: the moment we need multi-source routing, agent loops, or fast experimentation across rerankers and synthesis strategies, that's exactly the leverage LangGraph or LlamaIndex sells, and I'd rather adopt one than rebuild checkpointing and tracing myself.

interview lens — vector store name-drops

One sentence of landscape fluency: managed (Pinecone), open-source engines (Qdrant, Weaviate, Milvus), Postgres-native (pgvector — the "boring is good" pick when you already run Postgres), and search engines doing double duty (Elasticsearch/OpenSearch — dense kNN + BM25 + RRF in one system, which is exactly why your stack needed no extra vector DB).

module 07 / measurement

Evaluation & production engineering

Why interviewers ask: "how do you know it works?" separates people who demo from people who ship. This module is also your debugging vocabulary for the inevitable "our RAG is wrong, fix it" scenario.

The first principle: evaluate the two stages separately

An end-to-end "answer quality" number can't tell you which component to fix. Split it:

StageMetricWhat it tells you
Retrievalrecall@kDid the relevant docs make it into the top k at all? The ceiling on everything downstream.
precision@k / context precisionHow much of what you retrieved is actually relevant — and (RAGAS context precision) is the relevant material ranked high? Low score with good recall = ranking problem → reranker.
MRRWhere does the first relevant doc rank? Great for FAQ-style single-answer tasks.
nDCG@kPosition-aware, graded relevance. Correlates best with end-to-end quality; the standard before/after metric for reranker changes.
Generationfaithfulness / groundednessFraction of claims in the answer supported by retrieved context. Measures hallucination — not correctness: a faithful answer to bad context is still wrong.
answer relevancyDoes the answer address the question? (RAGAS trick: generate questions from the answer, embed, compare to the original query.)
answer correctnessAgreement with ground truth, when you have it. The end-to-end truth metric.

RAGAS mechanics worth knowing cold: faithfulness = LLM extracts atomic claims from the answer, verifies each against the context, score = supported/total. Context recall = can each sentence of the ground-truth answer be attributed to retrieved context (needs labels). Context precision rewards relevant chunks ranked above irrelevant ones. Noise sensitivity probes whether irrelevant retrieved content corrupts the answer. Citation metrics: track citation precision (cited sources actually support the claim) and citation recall (claims that have citations) separately.

Golden datasets & LLM-as-judge where eval data comes from
  • Golden set: 50–300 (query → relevant docs → reference answer) triples. Sources: real user queries (best), SME-written, or synthetic generation — LLM reads chunks and writes questions answerable from them (RAGAS testset generation does this, with evolution into multi-hop/reasoning variants). Synthetic sets skew easy: questions generated from a chunk are phrased like the chunk. Mix in real, adversarial, and unanswerable queries.
  • LLM-as-judge: scalable scoring of faithfulness/relevance with rubrics. Known biases — verbosity preference, position bias in pairwise comparisons, self-preference. Mitigate: pinned judge model + version, rubric prompts, randomized order, periodic human calibration audit.
  • Tooling: RAGAS (canonical metrics, dataset pipelines), DeepEval (pytest-style, CI-friendly), Promptfoo (A/B + red-teaming); tracing/observability via LangSmith, Langfuse, or Arize Phoenix. Eval in CI = retrieval regression tests run on every index or prompt change.
The failure-point taxonomy — your debugging map symptom → component → fix

Adapted from the "Seven Failure Points of RAG" line of work — this table is the answer to "our RAG gives wrong answers, what do you do?":

SymptomLikely failureFirst fixes
"I don't know" on answerable questionsMissing content or recall failureCheck doc actually ingested/parsed; hybrid retrieval; query expansion; raise k
Right doc exists, ranked too lowRanking failure (recall ok, precision bad)Add cross-encoder reranker; contextual enrichment; tune fusion weights
Retrieved chunk doesn't contain full answerChunking failure — answer split across boundariesParent-document / sentence-window; structure-aware chunking; bigger units
Hallucination despite good retrievalGeneration faithfulness failureGrounding prompt + citations; lower temperature; post-hoc NLI verification; abstain threshold
Correct but stale answersIndex freshness failureIncremental ingestion; recency-aware ranking; CRAG-style web fallback
Wrong format / ignored instructionsPrompt-assembly or output-contract failureStructured output schemas; separate instruction block from evidence block

The diagnostic procedure to narrate: reproduce → inspect the trace (what was retrieved, in what order, what entered the prompt) → classify against this table → fix the earliest failing stage → add the case to the golden set so it can't regress.

Production realities cost, latency, freshness, security
  • Latency budget: embed query (~10–30ms) + ANN search (~10–50ms) + rerank (~30–150ms) + LLM generation (the elephant: 1–10s). Optimize generation first (streaming, smaller models, caching), then retrieval.
  • Cost levers, in order: (1) retrieve less but better — k=6 with a reranker beats k=12 raw on both cost and quality; (2) model-tier routing — small model for easy queries, big for synthesis; (3) caching — semantic query cache, prompt-prefix caching, embedding cache.
  • Freshness: incremental upserts with stable chunk IDs (hash content), tombstone deletes, re-embed only changed chunks; reconciliation jobs against the source of truth. Embedding-model upgrades = full re-embed migration — version your vectors.
  • Security/ACLs: permission filtering at retrieval time (pre-filter by user's entitlements), never "retrieve everything, filter in the prompt." Multi-tenant isolation by namespace/partition. RAG also enlarges the prompt-injection surface: retrieved documents can carry adversarial instructions — sanitize, and treat retrieved text as data, not commands.
  • Monitoring: online proxies (retrieval scores distribution, abstain rate, citation coverage, thumbs feedback) + periodic offline eval on the golden set; alert on drift.
interview lens

My evaluation rule is: never a single score. Retrieval gets recall@k and nDCG against a golden set; generation gets faithfulness and answer relevancy, judged by a pinned LLM judge with spot human audits. The split matters because the fix differs — low recall means better first-stage retrieval or query expansion, low context-precision with fine recall means I need a reranker, and hallucination with good retrieval is a generation problem, not a search problem. Every production incident becomes a golden-set case, so the suite gets harder as the system ages.

module 08 / horizon

2026 trends & the "isn't RAG dead?" question

Why interviewers ask: to see if you follow the field and can hold a nuanced position under a provocative framing.

Long context vs RAG — the canonical answer

The provocation: "models take 1M+ tokens now; why retrieve at all?" The senior answer has four legs:

  • Economics: stuffing a million tokens into every query costs orders of magnitude more than retrieving the relevant 4k — and is slower. Caching helps repeated context, not heterogeneous corpora.
  • Scale: corpora are GB–TB; context windows are MB. The corpus will always be bigger than the window.
  • Attention quality: effective use of context degrades as it fills ("lost in the middle", needle-in-haystack failures at depth). More context ≠ more comprehension.
  • Governance: ACLs, freshness, and citation all require knowing which evidence supports the answer — which is retrieval by definition.

The synthesis: long context and RAG are complements. Long windows let you retrieve coarser units (whole documents, whole sections) and stress chunking less; retrieval remains the mechanism that decides what deserves the window. The fashionable umbrella term is context engineering — RAG is its retrieval arm.

What's actually moving in 2026

  • Agentic search as the default for hard questions — deep-research products normalize multi-step retrieve-reason loops; RAG pipelines increasingly expose themselves as tools for agents rather than end-user endpoints.
  • From RAG to memory systems — persistent agent memory (episodic, semantic, procedural) built on retrieval substrates; known failure mode to name-drop: memory consolidation that summarizes past interactions can introduce errors that compound ("the agent gets dumber as memory grows") and most stacks lack tooling to inspect or roll back memory state.
  • Multimodal RAG mainstreaming — vision-native retrieval (ColPali-style, Qwen3-VL embedding/reranker families, Cohere Embed v4) moving enterprise document QA away from lossy OCR pipelines.
  • Structured + neural hybrids — graphs, SQL, and symbolic filters fused with vectors; the "bag of isolated chunks" model acknowledged as incompatible with reasoning chains.
  • Evaluation maturing into CI — retrieval regression suites, citation metrics, cost-per-passing-query as the composite KPI.
interview lens

RAG-the-buzzword is fading; RAG-the-capability is being absorbed. The 2023 fixed pipeline is dissolving into agentic systems where retrieval is a tool, and into context engineering where the job is deciding what earns the window. But every leg of the original case — cost, corpus scale, attention degradation, permissions, citations — still stands, so retrieval isn't going anywhere. What's dying is the assumption that one static retrieve-then-generate pass is the architecture. I'd build today's system so the retriever is a clean, separately-evaluated service — that's the part that survives every architecture shift above it.

System design scenarios

Six interview-realistic prompts. For each: think out loud for 10–15 minutes before opening anything. Then compare against the clarifying questions, the model answer, and — most importantly — the rubric of what interviewers are actually listening for.

The answer skeleton that works for every scenario

Interviewers grade structure as much as content. Use the same five moves every time:

  1. Clarify the workload — corpus size/shape, query types, freshness, latency/cost budget, accuracy stakes, security. (2 minutes; signals seniority instantly.)
  2. Decompose — ingestion → retrieval → generation → evaluation. Name the hard sub-problem this scenario hides.
  3. Baseline first — propose the simplest credible pipeline; say what it gets right.
  4. Layer upgrades as trade-offs — "if X fails, I add Y, which costs Z." Every component must pay rent.
  5. Close with evaluation — golden set, retrieval metrics vs generation metrics, how the system improves over time.
scenario 01

Legal document QA with mandatory citations

the promptDesign a question-answering system over 10 million legal PDFs — contracts, case law, regulatory filings. Lawyers must receive answers with exact citations down to page and paragraph. A confidently wrong answer is worse than no answer.
Clarifying questions to ask first 2 minutes that buy credibility
  • Query mix: single-document lookups, or cross-document synthesis ("how have courts interpreted clause X")?
  • Latency tolerance — is 5–10 seconds acceptable for a high-stakes answer? (Almost certainly yes; quality dominates.)
  • What fraction of the corpus is scanned (OCR required) vs born-digital? Tables and exhibits?
  • Update cadence — daily filings? Are old versions of contracts superseded or co-existing?
  • Citation granularity contract: page + paragraph IDs from the source PDF?
  • Jurisdiction/date filters needed as first-class query parameters?
Model answer architecture + the decisions that matter

Hard sub-problem named up front: trustworthy citations at scale — which forces structure-preserving ingestion and verification at generation, not just good retrieval.

Ingestion. Layout-aware parsing (LlamaParse-class tooling or a layout model) + OCR lane for scans. Preserve structure: section hierarchy, clause numbers, page/paragraph anchors stored as chunk metadata. Chunk along legal structure — clauses and sections, never fixed windows; tables and exhibits extracted atomically. Add contextual enrichment per chunk (doc title, parties, jurisdiction, section path prepended before embedding — Anthropic-style contextual retrieval): at this quality bar the index-time LLM cost is justified and prompt caching keeps it sane.

Index & scale math. 10M documents at ~50–200 chunks each → call it 1–2B chunks: this is shard-and-partition territory. Partition by jurisdiction/doc-type (also your pre-filter), HNSW per shard if RAM allows, or IVF-PQ/DiskANN tiers for the cold tail. Hybrid channels: BM25 is non-negotiable — statute numbers, case citations, defined terms are exact strings embeddings blur — plus dense for paraphrase, fused with RRF; metadata pre-filters for jurisdiction and date.

Query side. Self-query extraction of filters (jurisdiction, date range, doc type); routing between lookup (direct hybrid) and synthesis (decomposition into sub-questions per document family).

Precision stage. Cross-encoder reranker over ~100 candidates → top 5–8, with a relevance floor: below threshold the system abstains ("no supporting authority found") rather than generating. Abstention is a feature here.

Generation with enforced citations. Chunks enter the prompt with stable IDs carrying page/paragraph anchors. The model must emit claim→ID mappings (structured output). Then a verification pass — NLI or LLM-judge — checks each claim against its cited span; unsupported claims are dropped or the answer abstains. Citations are verified, never trusted.

Evaluation. Lawyer-annotated golden set; retrieval recall@k and nDCG; citation precision (cited span supports claim) and citation recall (claims carrying citations) tracked separately; faithfulness; human review queue for low-confidence answers. Every escalation becomes a regression case.

What interviewers listen for the rubric
  • Scale arithmetic at the chunk level (docs × chunks), and partitioning as both a scale and filtering strategy.
  • An exact-match channel justified by the domain (legal identifiers) — hybrid as a reasoned choice, not a buzzword.
  • Citation enforcement as a mechanism (IDs + structured output + verification), not "the LLM will cite."
  • Abstention path and where the threshold lives.
  • OCR/parsing treated as a first-class risk, not a footnote.
  • Citation precision/recall named as separate metrics.
Red flags to avoid instant credibility loss
  • "Embed everything, top-k, done" — no structure preservation, no exact-match channel.
  • Taking LLM-generated citations on faith.
  • No answer for scanned documents or tables.
  • No abstain behavior in a domain where wrong > silent is catastrophic.
scenario 02

Enterprise assistant across Confluence, Jira, Drive, Slack — with permissions

the promptBuild an internal assistant that answers employee questions across Confluence, Jira, Google Drive, and Slack. Employees have different access rights, content changes constantly, and a permissions leak is a security incident.
Clarifying questions to ask first
  • Source of truth for permissions — IdP groups? Per-source ACLs? How fast must revocation propagate?
  • Freshness SLA per source (Slack minutes? Confluence hours?).
  • Query mix: how-to knowledge, project status, people/ownership questions?
  • Scale: documents per source, daily active users, peak QPS.
  • Audit/compliance requirements on what the assistant saw and said.
Model answer

Hard sub-problem named up front: ACL-correct retrieval under constant change. Everything else is standard; this is what you must not get wrong.

Ingestion. Per-source connectors on change feeds/webhooks → normalize into one schema: content, source, url, updated_at, acl_principals (users/groups allowed). Chunking per source type: Confluence pages → structure-aware by headings with heading-path prefixes; Drive docs similar; Jira tickets and Slack threads are short, self-contained units — document-level embedding is correct there (chunking them would fragment atomic content). One pipeline, per-type strategies.

The ACL design. Index ACL principals as chunk metadata. At query time, expand the user's identity to their group set from the IdP, and apply it as a pre-filter inside retrieval — the ANN/BM25 search itself only traverses authorized chunks. Never post-filter (selective filters can empty your top-k, and worse, unauthorized text must never even reach the reranker or prompt). Permission changes: event-driven metadata updates with a revocation SLA, plus a periodic reconciliation sweep against the sources. Multi-tenant style partitioning where group structure allows it.

Retrieval. Per-source hybrid (BM25 + dense, RRF) — then federated fusion across sources with source-aware weighting; recency boost for Slack/Jira where freshness ≈ relevance. Cross-encoder rerank on the merged set. Router for query classes (people questions → directory tool, not RAG).

Conversation & generation. Condense multi-turn history to standalone queries; answers carry source links and updated_at timestamps; audit log of retrieved IDs per answer.

Evaluation & the security test that impresses: automated ACL leak tests in CI — adversarial probes asserting user A can never retrieve content only user B may see, run on every index/code change. Plus per-source golden sets, freshness-lag monitoring (event → retrievable), and standard retrieval/generation metrics.

What interviewers listen for
  • Pre-filtering at retrieval time, stated explicitly, with the post-filter failure modes explained.
  • Revocation propagation + reconciliation — permissions as a pipeline, not a flag.
  • Per-source chunking judgment (short atomic items embedded whole — your project pattern, generalized).
  • Federated fusion and recency weighting across heterogeneous sources.
  • ACL leak testing as part of evaluation, not an afterthought.
Red flags to avoid
  • Filtering results after retrieval, or "instructing the model" not to reveal unauthorized content.
  • One chunk size for wiki pages, tickets, and chat threads alike.
  • Nightly full re-crawls as the only freshness story.
  • No audit trail of what evidence produced an answer.
scenario 03

Customer support chatbot — multi-turn, with escalation

the promptDesign a customer-facing support bot over product docs, FAQs, and resolved tickets. Conversations are multi-turn. The bot must hand off to a human when it isn't confident — silently wrong answers create churn.
Clarifying questions to ask first
  • Success metric: deflection rate, CSAT, resolution rate — and which dominates when they conflict?
  • Latency budget (chat UX usually wants first token < ~2–3s → streaming).
  • Can the bot access account data via tools, or knowledge-base only?
  • Languages? PII handling rules for the ticket corpus?
  • How is "not confident" allowed to behave — escalate, ask clarifying question, or both?
Model answer

Hard sub-problem named up front: multi-turn query understanding + a principled abstention/escalation path. The retrieval itself is standard.

Corpus design. Three collections, three unit strategies: product docs → structure-aware chunks with heading paths; FAQs → indexed as QA pairs — embed the question (queries match questions far better than answers), return the answer; resolved tickets → PII-scrubbed, filtered to resolution-bearing ones, embedded whole (short, self-contained — the document-level pattern again).

Conversational layer. Every turn: condense history + new message into a standalone query (the step most designs forget — "what about the Pro plan?" retrieves nothing raw). Then route: chitchat → no retrieval; product question → RAG; account-specific → API tools or escalate; frustration signals → human, immediately.

Retrieval & confidence. Hybrid + RRF across the three collections; cross-encoder rerank with a calibrated threshold. Below threshold → the bot says it isn't sure, asks one clarifying question or escalates with full context attached (conversation + retrieved candidates) so the human starts warm. Optimizing deflection quality over deflection rate is the stance to state.

Generation. Grounded prompt with doc links; guardrails on promises/refund policy; streaming; semantic cache for frequent questions (FAQ cache short-circuits the whole pipeline for the head of the distribution).

The flywheel. Thumbs + escalation outcomes feed the golden set weekly; track resolution rate, escalation precision (of escalated, how many truly needed humans), faithfulness on a sampled basis; CSAT per answered-vs-escalated cohort. The eval suite grows from production pain.

What interviewers listen for
  • History condensation named explicitly as a pipeline stage.
  • Routing before retrieval — not every turn deserves the vector DB.
  • FAQ-as-question-index insight; per-collection unit choices.
  • Escalation designed as a feature with a threshold and warm handoff, not a failure.
  • Feedback loop wired into evaluation; metrics that resist gaming (deflection quality).
Red flags to avoid
  • Retrieving on the raw last message in a multi-turn chat.
  • Answering account/billing specifics from documentation.
  • No abstention threshold — the bot always answers something.
  • Reporting deflection rate as the only success number.
scenario 04

Financial news QA — freshness is the product

the promptAnalysts ask questions about market-moving events. Sources: news wires and regulatory filings arriving continuously. Answers must reflect the latest information and must never silently serve stale facts.
Clarifying questions to ask first
  • Freshness SLO: how many seconds/minutes from publication to retrievable?
  • Temporal queries ("as of last Tuesday", "before the merger announcement") in scope?
  • How should conflicting reports be handled — prefer newest, prefer primary, or surface the conflict?
  • Numeric precision stakes (prices, EPS) — is paraphrase of numbers acceptable? (No.)
  • Is live web/search-API fallback allowed when the index lags?
Model answer

Hard sub-problem named up front: time. Treat event-time as first-class metadata, make freshness structural, and design an explicit staleness escape hatch.

Ingestion. Streaming pipeline: parse → enrich (tickers/entities normalized, event_time vs ingest_time both stored) → embed → upsert within the freshness SLO. Time-partitioned indexes: a hot index (last 24–72h, small, fast, re-rankable aggressively) and a warm/cold archive — recency boosts and TTL become index structure, not score hacks.

Retrieval. Hybrid (tickers and exact figures are BM25 territory; event paraphrase is dense territory) + RRF; self-query extracts time filters and entities ("Tesla this week" → ticker=TSLA, event_time within 7d); recency-aware ranking inside the window the query implies.

The CRAG layer — this scenario is where it earns its keep. A retrieval evaluator grades the candidate set for sufficiency and staleness relative to query intent. Confident → decompose-and-recompose strip refinement (keep only relevant sentences). Stale/insufficient → query-rewritten live search-API fallback. Ambiguous → merge both. The system never silently answers from an index it judged stale.

Generation. Every answer states its evidence timestamp ("as of 14:32 UTC, per Reuters…"); numbers quoted verbatim from sources with citations; conflicts surfaced explicitly ("Reuters reports X; the 8-K states Y") with primary sources preferred. As-of semantics in the prompt for temporal queries.

Evaluation. Freshness lag as a monitored SLO (publication → retrievable); staleness incidents become golden cases; faithfulness + numeric-accuracy spot checks; canary queries on scheduled events (earnings releases) to verify the pipeline catches them within SLO.

What interviewers listen for
  • Hot/warm index partitioning — freshness as architecture.
  • event_time vs ingest_time distinction; temporal filters from self-query.
  • A graded fallback path (CRAG pattern) with explicit staleness detection.
  • Answers that disclose their as-of time; conflict surfacing policy.
  • Freshness SLO with monitoring, not "we re-index often."
Red flags to avoid
  • One monolithic index rebuilt nightly for a minute-fresh product.
  • Recency handled only by a score multiplier with no time metadata.
  • Letting the model answer from parametric memory about breaking news.
  • No behavior difference when the index is known-stale.
scenario 05

Codebase QA over a 10M-line monorepo

the promptBuild an assistant for engineers: "where is rate limiting implemented?", "how does the billing service talk to invoicing?", "write a client for our internal config API." The repo changes with every merge.
Clarifying questions to ask first
  • Languages in the repo; do docs (READMEs, ADRs, runbooks) join the corpus?
  • Query mix: symbol lookup vs conceptual "how does X work" vs code generation?
  • Freshness: index on every merge to main? Branch awareness needed?
  • Latency target (IDE-adjacent → snappy) and where it runs (code can't leave the VPC?).
Model answer

Hard sub-problem named up front: code's meaning lives in structure and references, not prose similarity — so chunk by syntax and retrieve partly by graph.

Chunking. AST-aware: functions/classes as units, each carrying file path, signature, docstring, and its import context as metadata; oversized functions split at block boundaries. READMEs/ADRs chunked by headings. Never fixed 512-token windows over code — that severs definitions mid-body.

Retrieval — hybrid is non-negotiable here. Identifiers are exact tokens: RateLimiterV2 must hit via BM25/keyword (with identifier-aware tokenization: camelCase/snake_case splitting), while "how do we throttle requests" needs dense embeddings (code-tuned embedding model). Fuse with RRF — your fusion pattern, third domain.

Cross-file questions need a graph. "How does billing talk to invoicing" spans files no chunk covers. Build a lightweight code graph (imports, call edges, service interfaces from build metadata). Retrieval: text channels find seed symbols → expand 1–2 hops along the graph → rerank the expanded set. This is the LightRAG/HippoRAG idea applied where the graph is free — compilers already know it; no LLM extraction needed.

Freshness. Index incrementally per merge: changed files only, chunk IDs = path + content-hash, tombstone removed symbols. Full re-index only on embedding-model upgrades.

Generation. Answers cite file paths + line ranges; for "write code using our API" queries, retrieve usage examples — tests are gold (canonical, working invocations) — and pin to current signatures to avoid training-data drift of internal APIs.

Evaluation. Golden set from real dev questions (Slack #help mining); exact-symbol-lookup recall tracked separately from conceptual-question quality; "first correct file in top-3" as a UX-aligned metric; generated-code compile/test pass rate.

What interviewers listen for
  • AST/structure chunking with path+signature context attached.
  • Exact-identifier channel with code-aware tokenization, justified by failure modes.
  • Graph expansion for cross-file reasoning — and noticing the code graph is free.
  • Merge-time incremental indexing with content-hash IDs.
  • Tests-as-usage-examples; citations as file:line.
Red flags to avoid
  • Fixed-size text chunking over source code.
  • Dense-only retrieval where exact symbols dominate queries.
  • Nightly full re-embeds of a repo that merges hourly.
  • Code answers without file references.
scenario 06

"Our RAG gives wrong answers — you have two weeks"

the promptYou join a team running a naive pipeline (512-token chunks, single embedding model, top-5, straight into the prompt). Users report wrong answers, "I don't know" on covered topics, and invented policy details. Diagnose and fix it.
Clarifying questions to ask first
  • Is there tracing — per query, can I see what was retrieved, with what scores, and the final prompt?
  • Does any eval set exist? Labeled complaints? Can I mine tickets?
  • Complaint distribution: which of the three symptoms dominates?
  • Corpus shape: doc types, structure, size; who owns ingestion?
Model answer — narrate a procedure, not a shopping list

Step 1 — instrument before touching anything. Add tracing: query → retrieved chunks + scores + ranks → assembled prompt → answer. You cannot debug retrieval you cannot see.

Step 2 — build the golden set (day 1–3). 50–100 cases from complaint tickets + known-good queries, each labeled with relevant docs and a reference answer. This is the yardstick every change gets measured against.

Step 3 — bucket failures with split metrics. Run the set; compute recall@5, context precision/nDCG, faithfulness. The three reported symptoms map cleanly: "I don't know" on covered topics → recall failure (content missing, badly chunked, or vocabulary mismatch); wrong-but-plausible answers → ranking failure (right doc exists, ranked below the cut) or context fragmentation; invented policy details → faithfulness failure at generation.

Step 4 — fix in causal order, one change at a time, re-running eval after each.

  • Ingestion audit: are the complained-about docs even ingested and parsed correctly? (Embarrassingly often: no.)
  • Chunking: structure-aware splitting with heading paths; parent-document retrieval so fragments stop masquerading as answers.
  • Retrieval: add BM25 alongside dense, fuse with RRF — fixes exact-term misses (policy names, codes) that pure vectors blur. Raise first-stage k to ~50.
  • Ranking: cross-encoder reranker 50 → 5; measure nDCG before/after to prove it pays.
  • Generation: grounding prompt with citations required; relevance floor → honest abstention with escalation instead of fluent invention.

Step 5 — lock it in. Ship behind an A/B, promote the golden set into CI as a regression gate, and route every new production failure into the set. Close by naming expected wins per change — hybrid fixes the exact-term misses, parent-doc fixes fragment answers, the reranker fixes "right doc at rank 12," abstention converts hallucinations into handoffs.

What interviewers listen for
  • Observability and an eval set before any fix — discipline over heroics.
  • Symptom → component mapping using separate retrieval/generation metrics.
  • Causal-order fixes, one variable at a time, measured each step.
  • Cheap wins first (parsing, chunking, hybrid) before exotic architecture.
  • Regression suite + feedback loop as the ending, so it can't rot again.
Red flags to avoid
  • "First I'd swap the embedding model / add GraphRAG" — solution before diagnosis.
  • Prompt-tweaking your way around a retrieval problem.
  • Changing five things at once with no eval between.
  • No story for keeping it fixed (CI, feedback loop).
